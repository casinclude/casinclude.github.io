Diversity

There is no doubt that the career opportunities for Computer Science graduates are plentiful. Employers continue to actively seek out graduates from under-represented minorities and a wide variety of backgrounds in an effort to diversify their workforce.

The problem lies in convincing students from underrepresented groups to join up and see value in STEM courses at school. We believe that excellent teachers with access to the latest research on diversity can make a huge difference.

Action Plan: Pupil Premium

1 – Identify your disadvantaged students

In the United Kingdom, there is a Department for Education grant known as Pupil Premium. Your school data manager or senior leadership team should be able to let you know the names of Pupil Premium students in your school.

2 – Identify barriers

Sir John Dunford has written an article on supporting Pupil Premium students and lists the common barriers to learning. It is important to understand that every Pupil Premium student is different and has their own set of needs. They may be incredibly able in Computing and have an excellent work ethic that allows them to perform at a high level without much additional support at all. Pupil Premium doesn’t automatically mean low ability. This is a misconception that needs to be addressed.

3 – Remove barriers

There might be specific barriers that relate to Computing.

  • Do they have access to a computer at home?
  • Do they have internet access?
  • Can they focus on homework?
  • Do they need extra support for practising coding?
  • Do they have secure foundations for studying Computing?

4 – Use successful teaching and learning strategies

This is the KEY to success with Pupil Premium students in your care. Successful strategies can be found in the Teaching and Learning Toolkit created by the Education Endowment Foundation
